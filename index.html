ABOUT THE ICE CORE

The model displayed here is based on the ice core drilled by the Greenland Ice Sheet Project 2 (GISP2). The actual core is three kilometers (1.9 miles) long; this model is three meters (9.8 feet) long.

GISP2 is one of several long ice cores drilled into the Greenland Ice Sheet, which together document nearly 130,000 years of
Greenland climate.

Data from these and other ice cores from around world inform our understanding of past climate at the regional, hemispheric and global scale. As ice sheets and glaciers melt due to human-caused climate change, these records could be lost forever.

ABOUT THE DATA

As you move along the ice core, you can see how temperature, carbon dioxide and other factors have changed over the last 110,000 years. Where are these data from?

Temperature: Data are from studies of Greenland ice cores using ratios of various isotopes in the ice to derive past temperature. For example, low ratios of light to heavy oxygen isotopes indicate cooler temperatures; higher ratios reflect warmer periods.

Global carbon dioxide: Air bubbles trapped in ice can be analyzed for climate data, including past atmospheric CO₂ levels. The best ice-core CO₂ records are from Antarctica, where the snow is uncontaminated by other forms of carbon. Because greenhouse gases are mixed throughout the atmosphere, Antarctic records accurately reflect global CO₂ concentrations. Data are from Antarctic ice cores drilled at Law Dome, EPICA Dome C and Vostok Station.

Data after 1958 are direct measurements from the Mauna Loa Observatory in Hawai’i.

Global sea level: These data are drawn from studies of marine sediment cores containing the remains of shelled organisms. Oxygen isotope ratios in these remains can be used to reconstruct past sea levels. Changes in the global average sea level do not necessarily apply to individual coastlines.

Estimated human population: Pre-1950 population estimates are from the HYDE 3.1 database maintained by the Netherlands Environmental Assessment Agency. More recent data are from the UN Population Division.

Resolution of data: As data go back further in time, the resolution of data decreases and error bars increase.
